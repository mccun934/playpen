01—10

Ten principles, applied to the work.

Dieter Rams wrote ten principles for good design. They describe how I try to lead just as well as how Braun built a radio. The product here is the team.

01

Is innovative

The work is never finished. New tools and ideas — AI now most of all — keep opening better ways to build. I adopt them when they serve the team and the product, never for novelty's own sake.

02

Is useful

A team exists to ship. It has to satisfy more than a roadmap — it has to work for the people on it and the customers it serves. I remove anything that gets in the way of that.

03

Is aesthetic

How an org is built is part of whether it works. The systems people touch every day — clarity, trust, tools, ritual — shape their well-being and their output. Only well-made ones endure.

04

Is understandable

Good structure explains itself. The strategy, the priorities, and each person's part in them should be legible without a meeting to decode them. At best, the work is self-evident.

05

Is unobtrusive

A leader is a tool, not the point. My job is to build the system around the team and then get out of its way — leaving room for people to do their best work.

06

Is honest

No inflated metrics, no manufactured urgency. I won't make the work look more finished or more certain than it is. I'd rather commit to less and deliver it.

07

Is long-lasting

I build for durability, not for the quarter. Teams that scale themselves out of the critical path outlast any trend, reorg, or hero — and never appear antiquated.

08

Is thorough to the last detail

Nothing arbitrary. Observability, on-call, and incident hygiene — the unglamorous parts — treated as first-class work. The care shows where no one is looking. That is respect.

09

Is environmentally friendly

Conserve what's scarce: people's time, attention, and trust. I cut waste — meetings, busywork, infra cost (−50% in one org) — so energy goes where it actually matters.

10

Is as little design as possible

Less, but better. Ruthless prioritization, tight feedback loops, one observable bet at a time. Strip away everything inessential until only what ships is left.
